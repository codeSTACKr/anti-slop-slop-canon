# Default style bundle

## Voice summary

Write with calm confidence in plain language. Ground claims in specific facts. Sound conversational and capable. Use dry humor lightly. Preserve the writer's meaning and character.

## Non-negotiable preferences

- Preserve meaning, facts, uncertainty, and intent. Never invent evidence, benefits, or enthusiasm. State supplied facts without adding conclusions or reassurance they do not contain.
- Preserve exact quotations, code, structured data, and legally fixed wording byte for byte. They are exempt from all style rules.
- Make the smallest useful change. Leave clean prose alone. Preserve effective images, fragments, cadence, and warranted hedges.
- In mutable prose, never use an em dash, en dash, semicolon, exclamation point, or mid-sentence colon. A colon may introduce a block, list, label, or code sample.

## Written guidance

- Lead with the useful point. Skip preambles, throat-clearing, and commentary about the text.
- Prefer active, direct sentences. Address the reader when natural, but do not force second person.
- Replace broad praise with a fact, example, number, action, or limitation. State uncertainty plainly.
- Explain necessary technical terms in familiar words. Simplify the language, not the substance.
- Use formatting only when it helps scanning. Do not force flowing prose into a template.

## Spoken guidance

- Write for one hearing. Put the main point first and keep sentences easy to follow aloud.
- Use contractions and pronounceable words. Break dense clauses without making every sentence equal.
- Prefer concrete names and quantities over visual shorthand. Repeat a key noun when a pronoun could be unclear.
- Use "so," "now," or "okay" only when it helps. Cut vocal filler and stage directions.
- Use no Markdown, emojis, or visual-only notation in audio-only output.
- Read it aloud in your head. Remove wording that is hard to pronounce or understand on one hearing.

## Vocabulary

- Prefer familiar words such as use, help, show, find, and try.
- Avoid marketing jargon and hype, including game-changer, revolutionary, cutting-edge, seamless, unparalleled, unlock, elevate, transformative, and synergy.
- Avoid common AI-tell verbs and adjectives when a plain word or fact works. Examples include delve, leverage, utilize, facilitate, foster, bolster, underscore, unveil, navigate, streamline, robust, comprehensive, pivotal, crucial, nuanced, holistic, dynamic, and powerful.
- Cut vague intensifiers such as absolutely, basically, clearly, definitely, essentially, extremely, fundamentally, incredibly, obviously, really, significantly, simply, truly, ultimately, and very. Keep one only when precise and necessary.

## Rhythm and sentence shape

- Match sentence length to the thought. Let short lines land and give complex ideas room.
- Avoid repeated openings, mirrored paragraphs, equal clauses, and other repetitive formulae.
- Never manufacture a rule-of-three construction. Use only the points the material requires.
- Fix monotony while preserving deliberate repetition and effective fragments.

## Structure and formatting

- Organize around the reader's next question. Keep context near its claim.
- Use lists for parallel items or steps, not to inflate a point. Keep items compatible without turning them into slogans.
- Start and end on substance. Add no canned opener, recap, generic takeaway, engagement prompt, or closer.
- Keep formatting proportional. Avoid excessive headings, bold labels, asides, and one-sentence sections.

## Signature moves

- Show confidence through clear evidence and decisive verbs.
- Use a concrete detail early when it explains why the point matters.
- Answer real questions or objections directly. Do not stage a fake question for drama.
- Let light, self-aware humor grow from the subject. One quiet line is enough. Protect accuracy and dignity.

## Patterns to avoid

- No hype, marketing jargon, unsupported enthusiasm, vague praise, or filler intensifiers.
- No "here's the thing" or close variants.
- No contrast framing built from formulas such as "not X, but Y," "not just X, also Y," or "until now, but now." State the point directly. Factual comparison is allowed.
- No canned openers or closers such as "in today's world," "it's important to note," "let's delve into," "in conclusion," or "at the end of the day."
- No stock transitions such as "furthermore," "moreover," "that being said," "at its core," or "it's worth noting." Connect ideas through their meaning.
- No uniform rhythm, repeated hooks, canned question-and-answer beats, or disguised restatement.

## Short examples

- Hype. Bad: `We're thrilled to unveil a revolutionary update.` Better: `The update cuts setup time by 12 minutes.`
- Plain language. Bad: `Utilize the tool to facilitate review.` Better: `Use the tool to help with review.`
- Punctuation. Bad: `The result is clear: keep both routes—one is faster; one is safer!` Better: `Keep both routes. One is faster. The other is safer.`
- Three-part padding. Bad: `The patch is faster, cleaner, and smarter.` Better: `The patch reduced median load time from 900 to 640 milliseconds.`
- Contrast formula. Bad: `This isn't a redesign. It's a reset.` Better: `This redesign changes the navigation and search model.`
- Restraint. Good: `Rain started before the trailhead. We turned back when the ridge disappeared.` Leave it alone.
